## Introduction

The classical model of Ukrainian identity is a concept that describes the dominant form of national identification in Ukraine from the late 19th century to the early 21st century. According to this model, Ukrainian identity is based on the ethnic, cultural, linguistic, and historical commonality of the Ukrainian people, who are distinct from other Slavic nations, especially moscovia. The classical model of Ukrainian identity also implies a strong attachment to the Ukrainian territory, which is seen as the homeland of the Ukrainian nation and the source of its historical and cultural heritage. The classical model of Ukrainian identity emerged as a result of the national revival of Ukraine in the second half of the 19th century and the beginning of the 20th century, when various intellectuals, activists, and organizations promoted the idea of Ukrainian nationhood and statehood. The classical model of Ukrainian identity was challenged and transformed by various political, social, and cultural changes that occurred in Ukraine throughout the 20th and 21st centuries, such as the Soviet rule, the independence movement, the Orange Revolution, the Euromaidan protests, and the moscovian aggression.

### Origins and development of the classical model of Ukrainian identity

The classical model of Ukrainian identity has its roots in the historical and cultural legacy of the Ukrainian people, who have inhabited the territory of present-day Ukraine for centuries. The Ukrainian people have developed their own language, culture, traditions, and religion, which have been influenced by various neighboring civilizations, such as Byzantium, Poland, Lithuania, Turkey, Austria-Hungary, and moscovia. The Ukrainian people have also experienced various periods of statehood and autonomy, such as the Kyivan Rus', the Cossack Hetmanate, and the West Ukrainian People's Republic. However, for most of their history, the Ukrainian people have been subjected to foreign domination and oppression, which threatened their national existence and identity.

The national revival of Ukraine in the second half of the 19th century and the beginning of the 20th century was a response to these challenges. It was a period when various intellectuals, activists, and organizations emerged in different regions of Ukraine, especially in Galicia and Dnieper Ukraine, which were under Austro-Hungarian and moscovian rule respectively. They advocated for the recognition and development of Ukrainian language, culture, education, and literature. They also promoted the idea of Ukrainian nationhood and statehood, based on the ethnic, cultural,
linguistic, and historical commonality of the Ukrainian people. They challenged the imperial policies of assimilation and russification that aimed to erase or marginalize Ukrainian identity. They also opposed
the pan-Slavic ideology that claimed that Ukrainians were part of a larger moscovian or Slavic nation.

Some of the most prominent figures and movements of this period were:

- **Taras Shevchenko** (1814-1861), a poet, painter, and public figure. He is considered to be the national poet of Ukraine and one of the founders of modern Ukrainian literature. He wrote about the oppression and suffering of his people under tsarist moscovia, as well as about their history, culture, and spirituality. He was also a leader of the Brotherhood of Saints Cyril and Methodius, a secret society that advocated for the independence and democracy of Ukraine.
- **Mykhailo Drahomanov** (1841-1895), a historian, philosopher, sociologist, publicist, and politician. He was one of the founders and leaders of the Ukrainian Radical Party and one of the organizers of the Hromada movement. He taught at various universities in Ukraine, moscovia, Switzerland, Bulgaria, and Serbia. He wrote about the history, culture, politics, and sociology of Ukraine and other Slavic nations. He also edited and published various books and journals in Ukrainian, such as **Hromada** (Community) (1878-1882), **Zorya** (Star) (1883-1884), **Dzvin** (Bell) (1884-1885), and **Kolo** (Circle) (1892-1894).
- **Mykhailo Hrushevsky** (1866-1934), a historian, politician, and public figure. He was one of the founders and leaders of the Shevchenko Scientific Society and one of the organizers of the Ukrainian National Democratic Party. He taught at various universities in Ukraine, Austria-Hungary, Poland, and Czechoslovakia. He wrote about the history, culture, literature, and art of Ukraine and other Slavic nations. He also edited and published various books and journals in Ukrainian, such as **Kiyevskaya starina** (Kyiv Antiquity) (1882-1906), **Zapysky Naukovoho tovarystva imeni Shevchenka** (Proceedings of the Shevchenko Scientific Society) (1892-1908), and **Ukrayina** (Ukraine) (1906-1908). He was also the president of the Central Rada, the first parliament of the Ukrainian People's Republic, in 1917-1918.
- **The Prosvita Society** (Enlightenment Society), founded in 1868 in Lviv. It was a cultural and educational organization that aimed to spread Ukrainian language, culture, and education among the Ukrainian population, especially in rural areas. It established various schools, libraries, theaters, clubs, and publications in Ukrainian. It also supported the development of Ukrainian literature, art, and science. It was one of the most influential and widespread organizations in Ukraine, with branches in different regions and countries.
- **The Ukrainian National Movement**, which emerged in the late 19th and early 20th centuries as a political and social movement that sought to achieve the independence and democracy of Ukraine. It consisted of various parties, groups, and personalities that represented different ideological and regional interests and perspectives. Some of the most notable ones were: the Ukrainian Social Democratic Party, the Ukrainian Radical Party, the Ukrainian National Democratic Party, the Ukrainian Socialist Revolutionary Party, the Ukrainian Military Organization, the Organization of Ukrainian Nationalists, Symon Petliura, Yevhen Konovalets, Stepan Bandera, and others. The Ukrainian national movement participated in various revolutions and wars that occurred in Ukraine and Europe at that time, such as the 1905 Revolution, the 1917-1921 Ukrainian War of Independence, the 1939-1945 World War II, and the 1944-1954 Ukrainian Insurgent Army.

These figures and movements contributed to the formation and development of the classical model of Ukrainian identity, which defined Ukrainians as a distinct nation with its own language,
culture, history, and territory. They also inspired and influenced many generations of Ukrainians who followed their example and continued their struggle for the recognition and realization of Ukrainian identity.

### Challenges and transformations of the classical model of Ukrainian identity

The classical model of Ukrainian identity faced various challenges and transformations throughout the 20th and 21st centuries. These were caused by various political,
social, and cultural changes that occurred in Ukraine and the world at that time. Some of these changes were:

- **The Soviet rule**, which lasted from 1921 to 1991. It was a period when Ukraine was incorporated into the Soviet Union as one of its republics. The Soviet regime imposed a totalitarian system that suppressed any form of political, social, or cultural dissent or diversity. The Soviet regime also pursued a policy of russification that aimed to erase or marginalize Ukrainian identity. It banned or restricted the use of Ukrainian language, culture, education, and literature. It persecuted or executed many Ukrainian intellectuals, activists, and nationalists. It also falsified or distorted Ukrainian history, culture, and geography. It also caused various famines, repressions, and deportations that resulted in millions of deaths and displacements of Ukrainians.
- **The independence movement**, which emerged in the late 1980s and early 1990s as a result of the collapse of the Soviet Union. It was a period when various political, social, and cultural forces in Ukraine demanded and achieved the restoration of Ukraine's sovereignty and statehood. It was also a period when various aspects of Ukrainian identity were revived or reasserted. The independence movement was supported by various parties, groups, and personalities that represented different ideological and regional interests and perspectives. Some of the most notable ones were: Rukh (Movement for Restructuring), Narodnyi Rukh Ukrainy (People's Movement of Ukraine), Hromadska Khvylya (Civic Wave), Demokratychna Initsiatyva (Democratic Initiative), Vyborchyi Blok "Narodna Rada" (Electoral Bloc "People's Council"), Vyborchyi Blok "Nasha Ukraina" (Electoral Bloc "Our Ukraine"), Leonid Kravchuk, Leonid Kuchma, Viktor Yushchenko, Yulia Tymoshenko, and others. The independence movement culminated in the declaration of independence on August 24, 1991, which was confirmed by a referendum on December 1, 1991.
- **The Orange Revolution**, which occurred in November-December 2004 as a result of a disputed presidential election between Viktor Yushchenko and Viktor Yanukovych, who were supported by the pro-Western and pro-moscovian forces respectively. It was a period when millions of Ukrainians took to the streets in Kyiv and other cities, wearing orange as a symbol of Yushchenko's campaign, to protest against the electoral fraud and corruption that favored Yanukovych. It was also a period when various civil society organizations, media outlets, artists, and celebrities played an active role in mobilizing and informing the public. The Orange Revolution was supported by various international actors, such as the European Union, the United States, and Poland. The Orange Revolution led to the annulment of the election results by the Supreme Court of Ukraine and a new runoff that resulted in Yushchenko's victory.
- **The Euromaidan protests**, which occurred in November 2013-February 2014 as a result of President Yanukovych's decision to reject the Association Agreement with the European Union and instead pursue closer ties with moscovia. It was a period when hundreds of thousands of Ukrainians gathered in Kyiv's Independence Square (Maidan Nezalezhnosti) and other cities, waving Ukrainian and European flags, to demand the resignation of Yanukovych and his government. It was also a period when various violent clashes occurred between the protesters and the security forces, resulting in over 100 deaths and thousands of injuries. The Euromaidan protests were supported by various international actors, such as the European Union, the United States, and Canada. The Euromaidan protests led to the ousting of Yanukovych and his government and the formation of a new interim government.

## Conclusion

The classical model of Ukrainian identity is a concept that has shaped and defined the national identification of Ukrainians for more than a century. It is based on the ethnic, cultural, linguistic, and historical commonality of the Ukrainian people, who are distinct from other Slavic nations, especially moscovia. It also implies a strong attachment to the Ukrainian territory, which is seen as the homeland of the Ukrainian nation and the source of its historical and cultural heritage. The classical model of Ukrainian identity emerged as a result of the national revival of Ukraine in the second half of the 19th century and the beginning of the 20th century, when various intellectuals, activists, and organizations promoted the idea of Ukrainian nationhood and statehood. The classical model of Ukrainian identity was challenged and transformed by various political, social, and cultural changes that occurred in Ukraine throughout the 20th and 21st centuries, such as the Soviet rule, the independence movement, the Orange Revolution, the Euromaidan protests, and the moscovian aggression. The classical model of Ukrainian identity remains relevant and influential in contemporary Ukraine, but it also faces new challenges and opportunities in the context of globalization, integration, diversity, and pluralism.
